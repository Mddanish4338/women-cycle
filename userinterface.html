Menstrual
cycle

Menstruation is a normal and natural process that occurs in the reproductive system of women. It is the shedding of the lining of the uterus (endometrium) that occurs approximately every 28 days, marking the beginning of a new menstrual cycle. During this time, a woman may experience menstrual bleeding, which typically lasts for 2 to 7 days. Menstrual bleeding is the result of hormonal changes in the body. The hormones estrogen and progesterone work together to build up the endometrium, preparing it for a possible pregnancy. If pregnancy does not occur, the levels of these hormones drop, causing the endometrium to break down and be expelled from the body. Common menstrual problems include heavy bleeding (menorrhagia), painful periods (dysmenorrhea), irregular cycles, missed periods (amenorrhea), and frequent periods (polymenorrhea). These can be caused by various factors, including hormonal imbalances, stress, thyroid problems, uterine fibroids, endometrial hyperplasia, polycystic ovary syndrome, and endometriosis. It is important to seek medical attention if you are experiencing unusual or persistent menstrual problems, as they can sometimes indicate underlying health issues that need to be addressed. Your doctor may recommend various treatments, such as hormonal birth control, pain relievers, or surgery, depending on the cause of your symptoms.

Pain and Cramps
During Period

Painful periods, also known as dysmenorrhea, are a common experience for many women. Menstrual cramps are caused by the contraction of the uterus, which is necessary to help shed the endometrial lining. However, these contractions can cause discomfort and pain in the lower abdomen. The severity of menstrual cramps can vary from woman to woman and can range from mild to severe. In addition to abdominal pain, other symptoms may include back pain, headaches, nausea, and fatigue. There are several factors that can contribute to menstrual cramps, including hormonal imbalances, endometriosis, fibroids, or a condition called adenomyosis, where the tissue that normally lines the uterus begins to grow into the muscle wall of the uterus. Treatment for menstrual cramps can vary depending on the cause and severity of the pain. Over-the-counter pain relievers such as ibuprofen and naproxen can be effective for relieving menstrual cramps. Heating pads or warm baths can also help to relieve cramps. If the pain is severe or persistent, your doctor may recommend hormonal birth control or other medical treatments. It is important to seek medical advice if your menstrual cramps are severe, persistent, or interfere with your daily activities. Your doctor can help diagnose the cause of your symptoms and recommend appropriate treatment.

There are several types of medicine that can be used to treat menstrual problems, including: Pain relievers: Over-the-counter pain relievers such as ibuprofen and naproxen can help relieve menstrual cramps and other periodrelated pain. Hormonal birth control: Hormonal birth control can help regulate menstrual cycles and reduce the severity of menstrual cramps. Moderate exercise can be helpful for women during their menstrual cycle, as it can help reduce cramps, relieve stress, and improve overall mood. However, it is important to listen to your body and adjust your routine accordingly during this time. Here are some types of exercise that are often recommended during menstruation: Gentle yoga: Gentle yoga poses, such as forward bends and gentle twists, can help relieve cramps and promote relaxation. Light cardio: Light cardio exercises, such as walking or cycling, can help increase blood flow and reduce cramps. Strength training: Light strength training exercises, such as weightlifting or resistance band exercises, can help relieve menstrual cramps and improve overall strength and stamina. Swimming: Swimming can be a low-impact form of exercise that can help relieve cramps and provide a sense of weightlessness. It's important to keep in mind that everyone is different and what works for one person may not work for another. If you experience discomfort or pain during exercise, it's best to stop and rest. It's also important to consult with your doctor before starting any new exercise routine, especially if you have any medical conditions or concerns.

Avoid cold and heavy foods: Cold and heavy foods, such as ice cream and fried foods, can slow down digestion and increase cramps and bloating.
